Philosophy of Education

Having worked for nearly a decade in education, I’ve recognized that while some may have loftier goals regarding administration or academia, my greatest desire will always be focused on what I can directly do for students, both inside the classroom and outside. This includes improving my own pedagogy to make the learning more accessible, meaningful, and long-lasting. My pursuit of a Master’s in Learning Technologies and Design is one way I hope to utilize current available resources to enhance learning outcomes. However, teaching focused solely on learning outcomes may seem like successful practice, but does not necessarily imply best practice.

When so many students are viewing education as a commodity, as opposed to an experience or a privilege or a gift, we educators have to structure our pedagogy in such a way that we believe will truly contribute to the betterment of all our students. With that as my aim I have structured my education around two principles:

1) Make learning relevant to student’s lives by highlighting big ideas and real world implications to instill genuine curiosity and appreciation for learning.

2) Structure the environment and experience of learning in order to foster the values and behaviors that will improve the well-being of students and make them positive contributors to society.

I. Relevant Learning

Middle school teacher Eva Marie Olinski in E. L. Konigsburg’s The View from Saturday mourns her realization that students have stopped asking “Now what?” and instead ask “So what?” In today’s post-modern culture built on instant gratification, I find myself constantly butting heads with similar questions: “Why do I need to know this?”, or even worse, “When will I ever need to use this?” The challenge then becomes to sell my students on the importance of my subject matter. To assume that my own enthusiasm and passion for the content could solely serve as the bridge would be foolish, so I must make sure to meet them half way. I do this by ensuring specific concepts are related back to big ideas in ways that show their constant relevance to everyday life. Whether it’s by discussing questions like “Why Doesn’t Your Butt Fall Through the Chair?”1 or “When You Burn Off That Fat, Where Does It Go?”2, by putting concepts in terms of ideas or questions that students can relate to, they can have a true appreciation for the material they are learning.

II. Positive Community

In a world that is becoming more and more about the individual, I often come back to a quote by fictional White House Communications Director Toby Ziegler:

“We’re a group. We’re a team...We win together, we lose together. We celebrate and we mourn together. And defeats are softened and victories sweeter because we did them together… You’re my guys and I’m yours, and there’s nothing I wouldn’t do for you”.

One benefit from the advancement of technology is the growing realization that we can always identify ourselves as part of larger communities. If we expect our students to live within that framework, it’s only appropriate that we structure our classrooms to emulate that dynamic. Collaboration is high valued within my class, and activities and discussions are structured so that the contributions of every individual are valued and appreciated. Students are expected to work together, and learn from and teach each other. The ability to work with people of different backgrounds, experiences, and abilities is something I hope all my students can take from my class and use in their future pursuits. Even more so, I hope the appreciation and respect of all people will be an enduring value for all students who will pass through my classroom.
